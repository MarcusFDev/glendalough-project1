The Monastic City


Glendalough is the home of sone of the most important monastic sites on the east coast of Ireland. The 'Monastic City' as it is commonly known as today, was founded by the famous St. Kevin in the 6th century. This area was said to have been developed over time since it's creation 100s of years ago.


Today the settlement consists of structural remains and the most impressive of these being the Round Tower which stands 30m high. The main grouping of ruined buildings can be located downstream near the tower itself.


Despite the age of the site, experiencing the place in person is a treat many people of all ages will undoubtfully enjoy. The Round Tower itself is an iconic structure and is perfect as a backdrop for photos. If you come to Glendalough you cannot miss checking it out for yourself!

The Board Walk


The Board Walk is a great way to take in the view of the lower lake at Glendalough.


Please note weather conditions could make parts of the boardwalk slippery and to proceed with caution during extreme weather events. Keep up to date with weather forecasts and be safe while on the walkway.

Hiking up the Spinc's Walk


There are many paths and hikes a visitor could take in Glendalough; but none are quite as rewarding as the Spinc Mountain trail. This hike is not for the faint hearted so casual walkers beware. This trail begins with the hardest part of the path however, if you can climb 600 steps of stairs up the mountain pass you are in for a treat. The view is spectacular and the walk is slowly down hill from there.


All hikers please be aware and come prepared. The Spinc's path is excellently sign posted so chances of getting lost are unlikely. It is important to note however, that weather conditions can make the climb potentially treacherous and it is advised that you come well prepared for that days forecasted weather. The walk takes about 3 hours to complete for the average visitor so plan your day accordingly!

The Glendalough Mines


Glendalough's history is as recent as it is old. The Glendalough mines were opened back in the 1790's where lead, zinc and silver were mined. Mining in the valley took place for well over 150 years and at the peak of the mining production upwards of 2,000 miners were employed at the site. This continued up until 1957 where they were closed. Today the mines remain sealed shut and many flooded over the 60 years of inactivity.


While the mines themselves may not be accessible to visitors of Glendalough, the Mining village ruins remain in the area. Rusted machinary can also be found scattered and abandoned which makes for a nice walk through not so distant Glendalough history. In fact this place to visit, is nearing the end of the Spinc hike.

Swimming in the Lakes


The lakes in Glendalough are as iconic as it's Round Tower. The sandy area at the eastern end of the Upper Lake is a popular spot for visitors to paddle and swim during the summer months.


All Visitors please be warned that the lake is deep with sudden depth changes with the shallows areas remain narrow and drop off suddenly at points. Anyone entering the lake is warned that they do so at their own risk. Children are advised not to swim or paddle in the lake and should never without close supervision from Adults who can swim. For safety and environmental reasons, inflatables are not permitted to be used in the lake. There is no lifeguard on duty however life rings are present along the lake shores.

Upperlake Picnic Spots


Glendalough is truly a day long adventure for all ages. There is no better way to spend a sunny day trip to the valley than to have a picnic at the upperlake.


All visitors please be kind to the nature of the park and other's experience and pick up any waste you may have and deposit it in provided park bins. Also please note for safety and environmental reasons, any fire and solid fuel barbecues are not permitted in the National Park. While gas barbecues are permitted, please use them responsibly.
